The six figures behind the essay on Chen Ning Yang's wealth.

A 1957 Nobel cheque, a father who was China's first PhD in number theory, three monetary regimes, and seventy years of compounding. Each figure here is reproducible from a CSV plus a single Python file, no proprietary data.

About

This repository accompanies the essay How Rich Is Chen Ning Yang?, a long-form attempt to put an honest number on the wealth of a 20th-century Nobel laureate and to surface the structural conditions that produced him. Each figure has a source CSV with provenance notes and a self-contained matplotlib script. Run make all and you get the same six PNGs you see below.

The point of publishing the data and the code, not just the images, is that several figures (notably Figure 1 and Figure 4) are deliberately models rather than measurements. Anyone who disagrees with a deflator choice, a growth rate, or a percentile bin can fork, tweak a CSV, and re-run. The caveats section at the bottom of this page lists which figures are stylized and where.

Figure 01

Yang Zhenning's estimated wealth trajectory, 1922–2025.

Counterfactual compound-growth model on a log axis. Nobel share (1957) plus Stony Brook salary deposits (1966–1999) plus later prizes, grown at 8%, 10%, and 10.5% CAGR scenarios, with documented charitable gifts deducted. The endpoint range of $10M–$50M is a model, not a measurement. The nearly vertical jump between PhD (1948) and Nobel (1957) is the most informative feature of this figure, not the right edge.

Figure 02

Academic, industry, and AI compensation gradient, 2024.

Eight tiers on a log axis: postdoc to senior AI research scientist at a frontier lab. The span exceeds 40×. A top-university endowed chair's ceiling sits roughly at the floor of a senior AI research scientist's total package. For Yang's generation of physicists, the top of the academic ladder, inflation-adjusted, was below the entry compensation of a mid-tier AI engineer today.

Figure 03

Nobel laureates' parental socioeconomic background.

Parental income (left) and parental education (right) percentile distributions for 739 Nobel laureates (1901–2023), compared against the contemporaneous general population baseline. The average laureate's parents sit at the 87th income percentile and the 90th education percentile. The top-5% region produces approximately 50–60% of all laureates. The asymmetry has held steady for a century.

Figure 04

The 1957 Nobel share, projected to 2025 under four deflators.

Yang's $20,177 share of the 1957 Nobel money, projected to 2025 USD under four different deflators. The same sum becomes $230K, $1.5M, $14M, or $30M, depending entirely on which yardstick you choose. CPI measures supermarket eggs. The S&P 500 measures what indexing would have done. Gold measures a hedge against monetary regime change. Shanghai real estate measures what the money would be worth if it had landed in first-tier Chinese property. Every deflator is a value judgment; none is "objectively correct."

Figure 05

Yang Zhenning's academic genealogy.

Two parallel mentorship chains converging at Yang Zhenning. The physics line runs Sommerfeld → Born / Heisenberg → Fermi → Teller → Yang. The mathematics line runs Dickson → Yang Wuzhi → Yang. Chen Xingshen (S.S. Chern), a former student of Yang Wuzhi at Tsinghua, later served as one of Yang Zhenning's mentors at Southwest Associated University, closing the loop in a single generation. The structure of two independent elite mentorship chains converging on one person is exceptionally rare in scientific history.

Figure 06

Lost Einsteins: inventor rate by parental income.

Reproduction of the central plot from Bell, Chetty et al. (2019, QJE). Inventor rate per 1000 children plotted against parental income percentile, with and without controlling for early math ability. A 6–10× gap between wealthy and median-income families persists even after restricting the sample to the top 20% of third-grade math scores. The red star marks Yang Zhenning's birth position in 1922 (estimated top 0.1% of the Chinese income distribution at the time). Same story as Figure 3, told from a different angle.

Reproduce all six figures

Every figure on this page is a build target. Clone the repo, install the matplotlib dependencies, and run make all. Output lands in output/ as 300 dpi PNG plus SVG vector backup.

$ git clone https://github.com/ktwu01/yang-zhenning-wealth-figures.git
$ cd yang-zhenning-wealth-figures
$ pip install -r requirements.txt
$ make all          # build all 6 figures into output/
$ make fig1         # or build one at a time

To disagree with a deflator or a growth assumption, edit the relevant CSV in data/ and re-run. Pull requests welcome on the source notes.

Where the figures are models, not measurements

  • Figure 1 is a model. Yang Zhenning never published audited net-worth figures. The trajectory is built from public biographical anchors (salary bands by tier, prize amounts, documented charitable gifts) compounded forward under three CAGR scenarios. The $10M–$50M endpoint is a range, not a point estimate, and it represents accumulated principal minus known major gifts.
  • Figure 3 uses stylized bins consistent with the numerical findings reported by Novosad et al. (2024). The exact within-bin shares are illustrative. The verified takeaways are the medians (87th income percentile, 90th education percentile) and the top-5% share (~50–60%).
  • Figure 4's deflators use public-record CPI and gold prices. The S&P 500 multiplier (~700×) is Shiller's long-run total return; the Shanghai housing multiplier (~1500×) is directional, extrapolating from the post-1990 privatization-era price index. Treat the housing bar as an order-of-magnitude argument, not a precise number.
  • Figure 5 is a curated subset of a much larger genealogy. Some edges (notably Fermi → Yang) reflect documented intellectual influence rather than the official PhD-advisor relation, which was Edward Teller.
  • Figure 6 reproduces the qualitative shape of Bell, Chetty et al. 2019. The bin-level numbers are stylized to match the published curve, not pulled from IRS microdata.
